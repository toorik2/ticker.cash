A decentralized BCH/USD ticker.

Chipnet · three covenants · ~1.7 kB on chain

Seven notaries co-witness prices from thirteen exchanges. Any publisher relays them to a covenant that commits the per-cycle median on Bitcoin Cash. There are no admin keys. The covenant is the only on-chain rule.

What this is

ticker.cash is a BCH/USD price feed implemented as three CashScript covenants — Oracle, PublisherSlot, Ticker — and a few hundred lines of off-chain plumbing. The on-chain code is ~1.7 kilobytes total, deployed once. There are no proxies, no admin paths, no upgrade buttons. Behavioural changes require deploying a new covenant at a new address; the existing chain remains a self-perpetuating UTXO line.

A consumer dApp reads the price by spending a small NFT (a "Ticker") in its own transaction. The act of spending welds the consumer's tx to the Oracle update that minted the Ticker — BCH consensus refuses to confirm the consumer in any chain history that excludes the update. The price isn't something the dApp looks up; it's something the dApp holds in its hand while it acts. That's the property the design is built around.

How it works in one cycle

Every minute or so, when funded, the fleet does this:

  1. Each of 13 publishers asks one of 7 notaries to co-witness a price from its assigned exchange. The notary independently fetches the price over TLS and Schnorr-signs (serverName, sourceId, price, ts, cycleSeq).
  2. The publisher refreshes its persistent PublisherSlot NFT via slot.attest(). The slot covenant verifies the notary's signature, the publisher's own Schnorr signature, the CN/SAN hash binding the sig to a specific exchange, and the strict-monotonic cycleSeq advance.
  3. Once seven slots are refreshed for the current cycleSeq, any publisher races to broadcast Oracle.update. The Oracle covenant consumes the seven slot inputs, position-checks the median across distinct publishers, re-emits each slot unchanged, and mints two fresh Ticker NFTs carrying the new price.
  4. First broadcast wins. The losers retry on the new tip. Then it happens again.

The race-winner gets no privilege from winning. They just pay the transaction fee. The covenant is the only judge of validity.

Where the trust sits

One pivot. The 7-key notary federation is the only off-chain trust assumption. Each notary independently fetches its assigned price over TLS and signs the resulting tuple with its Schnorr key. To corrupt the feed, you bribe at least four of those keys, held by independent operators.

Everything else is in script. No party — not even the original deployer — can:

  • Skip the ≥ 7 distinct-publisher quorum check.
  • Replay a stale notary signature — each slot's cycleSeq is strict-monotonic and the signed message includes cycleSeq verbatim.
  • Hide a cycle from observers — every transition is an on-chain transaction.
  • Mint additional slot NFTs after genesis — CashTokens consensus closes the slot category forever once the genesis outpoint is spent.
  • Run the cycle faster than 30 seconds — both the covenant and the daemon enforce the floor.

A colluding 4-of-7 federation can sign attestations for arbitrary prices. That's its complete adversarial reach. Even at full majority, it cannot bypass any of the above five constraints. This is a design that accepts trust where it has to — witnessing prices from TLS-fronted CEXes — and rejects it everywhere else.

Sources are operator-diverse: 9 USD-quoted spot markets, 2 USDC venues, 2 USDT venues. No operator family appears twice; no quote-currency dominates. A position-checked median across thirteen values absorbs up to six simultaneous outliers without bias.

Signatures

Six signatures appear from "an exchange quoted a price" to "your dApp's transaction settles." Three are off-chain Schnorr signatures verified per slot refresh — the load-bearing trust path. The other three are standard BCH input signatures that just pay for inclusion.

Why each binding matters

publisherPkh in the notary sig. A notary can't sign one bundle and have ten publishers reuse it. Each notary sig is for one publisher in one cycle.

hash160(serverName) in the publisher sig. A publisher can't ask the Coinbase-side notary and quietly relabel the price as Kraken's. The covenant cross-checks the hash against the slot's pinned sourceId.

cycleSeq in both sigs. A signature from cycle N can't be replayed at N+1. The slot's stored cycleSeq is strict-monotonic; advancing past N kills cycle N's signature forever.

No off-chain sig on Oracle.update. Anyone can broadcast the update tx. The covenant is the sole judge of validity. The race-winner's wallet sig (sig 4) just pays the fee.

Reading the price

Two paths. For dApps that act on the price, the on-chain spend is the only way to get atomic guarantees. For dashboards and UIs, the JSON API is fine.

On-chain

Spend a Ticker NFT as an input in your transaction. The Ticker is mutable, anyone-can-spend, and self-replicates via the Ticker covenant. Your tx becomes a chain-descendant of the Oracle.update that minted the Ticker.

input[0]   your consumer covenant
input[1]   Ticker NFT  (pinned to the Oracle category)
output[0]  your business output
output[1]  Ticker re-emit  (Ticker.cash enforces structural equality)
output[2]  optional change

Decode tx.inputs[1].nftCommitment as a 17-byte commit:

0x80 | seq(4) | lastTs(4) | medianPrice(8)

Little-endian throughout. medianPrice / 10⁸ = USD. Pin the Oracle category (LE-reversed) as a constructor arg so an attacker can't feed your contract a Ticker from a different oracle.

The reference consumer is contracts/examples/PriceGatedRelease.cash. ~64-byte script body; locks BCH, releases on price ≥ strike.

Off-chain

$ curl https://usd.ticker.cash/api/v1/price

No auth, CORS open. stub:true means either the backend can't read the chain (HTTP 503) or the cycle is older than 5 minutes (HTTP 200 with a stale value). Always check stub before trading on the number.

Running a node

Two daemons, both stateless. The notary signs prices; the publisher relays. Run one or both.

$ ticker-node --notary --slot N                # notary only
$ ticker-node --publisher --slot N \           # publisher only
    --notary-url https://notary-0.ticker.cash \
    ...×6 more...
$ ticker-node --notary --publisher --slot N    # bundled

A publisher burns about 12 k sats per cycle — slot refresh fee plus a fair share of Oracle.update when it wins the race plus Ticker dust. Each operator generates a 32-byte seed locally; all seven notary keys and 13 publisher keys derive from it via sha256(seed ‖ utf8(label)). There is no shared key material, no key ceremony, no allowlist beyond holding the genesis-minted slot for an assigned source.

What we'd change

Two known limitations of the current design, both honest:

The reference consumer is a barrier option, not a price gate. The example PriceGatedRelease.cash gates on lastTs >= minLastTs, but lastTs is the Ticker's mint-cycle time — a snapshot, not "now." Once any past Ticker satisfies the strike, the gate opens forever. For genuine "current price ≥ strike" semantics, the protocol needs a freshness witness — a single-use NFT minted alongside Tickers that consumers structurally burn on spend. Old cycles' witnesses don't exist anymore; freshness becomes UTXO scarcity instead of trusted data. Planned for v12.2.

Oracle.update accepts subsets. Today the covenant requires ≥ 7 attestations but doesn't require all available ones. A race-winner who sees all 13 attestations in mempool can pick the 7 whose median is most extreme and bias the published feed by ~0.1–0.2 % per cycle. The fix touches the variable-N design directly and is still open; current best path is to revert to fixed-N with sentinel slots for absent publishers.

Neither breaks the trust model as long as the federation doesn't collude, but both are real and worth fixing before mainnet. Both are tracked in github issues.
